\question{
  id        = {0016},
  title     = {To Be or Not to Be (Independent)},
  source    = {OBECON},
  year      = {2026},
  round     = {Seletiva IEO -- Phase A},
  language  = {English},
  topic     = {Macro},
  subtopic  = {Monetary Policy / Phillips Curve},
  type      = {objective},
  statement = {%
    The image below shows reports that President Trump once considered firing
    Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell over disagreements about monetary
    policy.

    \begin{center}
      \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{../images/q0016_fig1.png}
    \end{center}

    Suppose the economy has a natural unemployment rate of 5\% and that the
    short-run Phillips curve is given by:
    \[
      \pi = \pi^e - 0.5\,(u - u_N)
    \]
    where $\pi$ is the actual inflation, $\pi^e$ is the expected inflation,
    $u$ is the unemployment rate and $u_N$ is the natural rate. At first the
    economy has $u = 3\%$ and $\pi^e = 2\%$. After the announcement,
    $\pi^e$ rises to 4\%. Suppose the Fed maintains its current policy stance
    (unemployment unchanged at 3\% in the short run). What are the
    implications for inflation and unemployment in the short run and long run?
  },
  choices   = {%
    \item In the short run, inflation increases to 5\% while unemployment
          remains at 3\%; in the long run, unemployment returns to 5\% and
          inflation stabilizes at 4\%.
    \item In the short run, inflation decreases to 5\% while unemployment
          remains at 3\%; in the long run, unemployment returns to 5\% and
          inflation stabilizes at 4\%.
    \item In the short run, inflation increases to 5\% while unemployment
          remains at 3\%; in the long run, unemployment returns to 5\% and
          inflation stabilizes at 3\%.
    \item In the short run, inflation decreases to 5\% while unemployment
          remains at 3\%; in the long run, unemployment returns to 3\% and
          inflation stabilizes at 3\%.
    \item In the short run, inflation remains at 3\% while unemployment
          increases to 5\%; in the long run, unemployment returns to 3\% and
          inflation stabilizes at 4\%.
  },
  answer    = {A},
  solution  = {%
    \textbf{Short run} (Fed keeps $u = 3\%$, $\pi^e$ jumps to 4\%):
    \[
      \pi = 4 - 0.5\,(3 - 5) = 4 + 1 = \mathbf{5\%}
    \]
    Inflation rises from 3\% to 5\%; unemployment stays at 3\%.

    \textbf{Long run} (expectations adjust; economy returns to $u_N$):
    In the long run $u \to u_N = 5\%$ and $\pi = \pi^e$ (no surprises).
    Since the Fed does not tighten policy to fight the higher expectations,
    inflation expectations settle at 4\%, so:
    \[
      \pi = 4 - 0.5\,(5 - 5) = \mathbf{4\%}
    \]
    The long-run Phillips curve is vertical at $u = 5\%$ with
    $\pi = \pi^e = 4\%$.
    Answer: \textbf{(A)}.
  },
}
