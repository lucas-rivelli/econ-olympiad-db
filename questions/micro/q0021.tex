    Suppose the cellphone market in Obeconland consists of two types of goods:
    new and used cellphones. Buyers know that both new and used cellphones are
    available in the market but cannot determine the type of a cellphone before
    purchasing it. Buyers are willing to pay R\$~1,200 for a new cellphone and
    R\$~700 for a used one, while sellers are willing to sell a new cellphone
    for R\$~1,000 and a used one for R\$~500.

    Trades occur at prices determined by bargaining between buyers and sellers.
    Knowing that the proportion of new cellphones in the market is $\pi$
    ($0 \leq \pi \leq 1$) and that this proportion is known to buyers, mark
    the correct alternative.
  },
  choices   = {%
    \item If $\pi = 0$, the price of a cellphone in this market would be
          $P^* = \text{R\$}\ 700$.
    \item The market failure evidenced in the statement is called information
          asymmetry, and the phenomenon that arises in this example is known
          as moral hazard (since one market participant changes their behavior
          after carrying out the transaction).
    \item If $\pi < \dfrac{3}{5}$, the magnitude of the externality caused by
          the presence of used cellphones in the market prevents the sale of
          new cellphones, so the market only works for used cellphones, with an
          optimal price $P^*$, $500 \leq P^* \leq 700$.
    \item If $\pi > 0$, it means that there are new cellphones in the market,
          and that is enough to ensure that both new and used cellphones will
          be bought and sold.
    \item If buyers knew the type of the cellphone before purchase, only new
          cellphones would be traded.
